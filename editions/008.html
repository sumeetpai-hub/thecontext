Day 4: Iran's Retaliation Reaches Dubai, Congress Moves to Halt Trump's War
What Happened
Four days into Operation Epic Fury, the conflict has widened beyond Iran's borders. Iranian missiles and drones struck Dubai International Airport — the world's busiest — killing four and cancelling 70% of flights. Abu Dhabi, Kuwait, Doha and Bahrain were also hit. Three US soldiers are dead, the first American casualties of the operation, with five more critically injured. Strait of Hormuz traffic has dropped 70%, with Maersk suspending all Gulf sailings. Brent crude has crossed $120 a barrel. In Washington, Congress is moving on a war powers resolution to force a pullback — though Trump's veto makes it largely symbolic. Iran has formed a three-person interim leadership council pending election of a new Supreme Leader.
Why It Matters
  • The Dubai strikes mark a new phase — Iran is no longer just hitting military targets. Striking the world's busiest airport is a deliberate signal that civilian and economic infrastructure is now fair game. Every Gulf state hosting a US base is now a target.
  • The 70% collapse in Hormuz traffic is the economic shock the world feared most. Over 20 million barrels a day normally transit that strait. At $120 oil, every import-dependent economy — India, China, Japan, South Korea — is facing an inflation spike before a single alternative barrel has been loaded.
  • The war powers resolution matters even if it fails. It forces every Republican senator on record. That vote — and the coalition it reveals or fails to build — will define the political shape of this war for months.
Connecting the Dots
Trump has not given a single public address since the strikes began — no press conference, no Oval Office speech, no joint appearance with allies. He has communicated exclusively through social media video clips. That silence is itself a strategic posture: it denies Iran a stage, denies the media a forum, and keeps the narrative uncontested. Whether it holds as casualties mount is the open question of this week.
Watch For → The Senate war powers vote — and whether any Republican breaks ranks. That's the first real test of whether this war has domestic limits.

India's Plan B: Russian Oil, 74 Days of Reserves, and a Silence That Speaks
What Happened
PM Modi chaired an emergency Cabinet Committee on Security meeting Sunday night as the Hormuz crisis deepened. India has about 74 days of combined strategic and commercial crude reserves. Air India has suspended all Middle East flights. Officials are signalling Plan B: accelerated Russian crude buys via eastern routes, spot purchases from the US, West Africa and Latin America, and drawdowns from strategic reserves at Visakhapatnam, Mangaluru and Padur. Brent touching $120 has already pushed India's import bill estimates sharply higher, with analysts warning of rupee pressure, rising inflation and compressed margins for oil marketing companies. India's official diplomatic statement on the strikes calls for "respect of sovereignty and territorial integrity of all states" — careful wording that neither endorses nor condemns.
Why It Matters
  • 50% of India's crude and 60% of its LNG normally transit Hormuz. Every $1 rise in oil prices adds roughly $2 billion to India's annual import bill. At $120 crude, the fiscal math changes dramatically — fuel subsidies, inflation, the current account deficit, and the RBI's rate path are all now in play.
  • Russia becomes India's relief valve again. Indian refiners had recently reduced Russian crude buys under US pressure. That calculus has now reversed overnight. Floating Russian storage in the Arabian Sea — around 20–24 million barrels — is available at a week's notice. India will quietly pivot back.
  • The diplomatic silence is a position. India's refusal to take a side while simultaneously being one of the most exposed economies is a high-wire act. It protects relationships in the short term but stores up leverage questions for later — especially with Washington, which will want to know whose side India is on when the war ends.
Connecting the Dots
India's Plan B is real and viable in the short term. The 74-day buffer buys time. The Russian optionality exists. But it comes with a cost: going back to Russian crude after reducing it under US pressure will be noticed in Washington at exactly the moment India most needs American goodwill on trade, technology and defence. India is not choosing between oil and diplomacy — it is choosing how long it can delay making that choice.
Watch For → Monday's Sensex and Nifty opening — and whether the RBI makes any emergency statement on the rupee. Markets will price India's exposure faster than diplomacy can manage it.
◆ Thinking Angle
The war Trump isn't explaining

Four days in, the United States has killed a head of state, taken its first combat casualties, triggered the closure of the world's most important oil route, and sent global markets into freefall. The president has communicated through social media clips. No address to Congress. No prime-time speech. No press conference. In every previous major American military action — Gulf War, Kosovo, Iraq, Afghanistan — a president stood before the country and explained. Trump has not.

That silence is not an oversight. It is the posture. It denies the opposition a moment to respond to, denies allies a commitment to hold him to, and denies the war a narrative he doesn't fully control. It may also reflect something simpler: that this war was launched faster than the justification was prepared. India is watching all of this — calculating how long it can stay silent too, and what the bill for that silence will eventually be.
